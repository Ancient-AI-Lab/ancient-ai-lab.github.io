### About Ancient AI Lab

I started this project because of a question I couldn't let go of: did ancient civilizations have construction technologies we don't understand?

Not ancient aliens. Not crystal energy. Just a straightforward engineering question -- how did people cut and move 100-ton granite blocks with the tools available 4,500 years ago? The mainstream answer is "ramps and copper chisels." The alternative answer is "sound technology and lost knowledge." Both camps have strong opinions and almost zero math.

So I built a lab to do the math.

### What this actually is

Ancient AI Lab is a computational research pipeline. I use finite element simulations (SfePy and Gmsh), symbolic regression (PySR), and good old NumPy/SciPy to model the physics of every mechanism I can find -- acoustic radiation pressure, piezoelectric coupling, electromagnetic fields, spacetime curvature, you name it.

The entire pipeline is built with Claude Code on a MacBook Air M4. Every simulation is reproducible. Every notebook is on GitHub. Every assumption is stated explicitly. And when the math says a mechanism doesn't work -- which is most of the time -- I report that just as carefully as the positive results.

### What I've found so far

15 completed research phases. 173 passing tests. 4,501 lines of Python. The short version:

Acoustic radiation pressure produces about 0.1 newtons at 500 watts of water-coupled sound. That's real and measurable. But it's roughly 245,000 times too weak to lift a pyramid block.

Piezoelectric coupling cancels out in natural granite because the quartz crystals point in random directions. I can prove it mathematically using SO(3) symmetry. I wanted this to work. The math had other plans.

The Navy's gravitational mass reduction patent has a 22-24 order-of-magnitude gap between what electromagnetic fields can produce and what you'd need to curve spacetime. They spent over $500K testing it. The patent expired in 2023 when they stopped paying maintenance fees.

But vibration-based friction reduction actually works -- it's experimentally verified. And pyramid geometry does show 10.77x pressure amplification at resonant frequency, though only at a single narrow frequency band.

### Why I'm doing this

Because somebody should. There are real questions about ancient construction that deserve real physics -- not YouTube speculation and not academic dismissal. I test claims regardless of source. I don't care if an idea comes from a peer-reviewed journal or a Reddit comment. If it's a testable claim, I run the simulation.

When the math says no, I say no. When the math says maybe, I say exactly how much of a maybe it is. Every number has error bars. Every conclusion has stated assumptions. And every notebook is on GitHub for you to run yourself.

That's it. That's the lab.
